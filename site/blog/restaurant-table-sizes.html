Restaurant Table Sizes: The Complete Guide

The right table size depends on three things: how many people sit at it, what kind of venue it is, and the top and base you pair together. Here are the dimensions that work — for cafés, fine dining and bars.

Gharnish·Specification guide·7 min read

Standard sizes by cover count

Casual venues use slightly smaller tops to fit more covers; fine dining and bars use larger tops with a thicker edge for a more substantial feel and more place-setting room.

The difference is deliberate: a café 4-seater at 4 × 2 ft keeps aisles open in a busy room, while a fine-dining 4-seater at 4 × 2.5 ft (48 × 30 in) gives each guest more space and a more relaxed pace.

Choosing the table top

The top sets both the look and the cost. Prices below are per square foot, so a larger top costs proportionally more in material:

Ply-laminate is the workhorse — economical, light and available in many finishes. Marble, terrazzo, sintered stone and Corian step up the look and durability. The catch is weight: sintered stone and Corian are heavy, so they need a sturdier base.

Matching the base to the top

The base isn't only about style — it decides how many people the table seats and whether a heavy top stays stable.

  • Single pillar → 2-seater
  • Double pillar → 4-seater
  • Twin single pillars → 6-seater

Pair a heavier stone top with a heavier base. A delicate single pillar under a sintered-stone top will feel unstable; a substantial base under it feels solid and safe in a busy room.

Common questions

What is the standard restaurant table size?

A 2-seater is about 2 ft × 2 ft, a 4-seater is 4 ft × 2 ft (casual) or 4 ft × 2.5 ft (fine dining/bar), and a 6-seater is around 6 ft × 2.5 ft. Round tops of 2 ft, 3 ft and 3.5 ft diameter are common too.

What size table do I need for a café?

A 2 ft × 2 ft top with a standard 18 mm edge seats two and suits compact café layouts.

What's the best table top material for a restaurant?

Ply-laminate is the most economical and versatile. Marble, terrazzo, sintered stone and Corian cost more per square foot but look premium and last well — just pair the heavier stone tops with a sturdier base.

How does the base decide seating?

Single pillar suits a 2-seater, double pillar a 4-seater, and twin single pillars a 6-seater. Match heavier tops to heavier bases for stability.
