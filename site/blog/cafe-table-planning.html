Restaurant Planning

How Many Tables Fit in Your Café?

Before you buy a single chair, two numbers decide your seating: how much usable dining area you have, and how much space each cover needs. Here is the simple way to plan it.

Gharnish·Planning guide·6 min read

Every restaurant owner asks the same question while signing the lease: how many people can I actually seat here? Pack in too many tables and the room feels cramped and staff can't move; too few and you leave revenue on the floor every night. The good news is you can estimate it in two steps.

Step 1 — Find your usable dining area

Your total carpet area is not your seating area. Once you subtract the kitchen, service counter, storage, restrooms and entry, the part left for guests is usually 55 to 60 percent of the total in a café, and a little less in a full-service restaurant with a larger kitchen.

So a 1,200 sq ft café has roughly 700 sq ft of real dining area to plan around.

Step 2 — Allow the right space per seat

Each seat needs more than the chair itself — it needs its share of the table and the circulation space to sit down, get served and walk past. The tighter the format, the less space per cover.

Planning space per seat
FormatSpace per seat
Quick-service / fast-casual10–12 sq ft
Café / casual full-service12–15 sq ft
Fine dining18–20 sq ft
Bar / lounge seating14–18 sq ft

Now the maths is simple — divide your dining area by the space per seat:

  • 700 sq ft ÷ 14 sq ft per seat ≈ 50 seats
  • That's about 12–13 four-seater tables, or a more flexible mix of 2-seaters and 4-seaters.

Choosing your table mix

The average café party is two people, so a room built mostly from 4-seaters wastes space every time a couple sits down. A better default:

  • Lead with 2-seaters. They fill first and suit the most common party size.
  • Add a few 4-seaters along walls or in corners for families and groups.
  • Use push-together tops. Two 2-seaters that combine into a 4 give you flexibility without committing floor space.

Don't forget clearance

Seats per square foot only works if people can move. Keep these minimums:

  • ~18 in (45 cm) behind a seated guest to push their chair back.
  • ~36–44 in (90–110 cm) for main walkways and the path to the kitchen.
  • ~24 in (60 cm) between the backs of chairs at adjacent tables.

If the clearances don't fit, reduce table count before you reduce aisle width — a tight aisle slows every plate that leaves the kitchen.

Common questions

How much floor space does each seat need?

Around 10–12 sq ft per seat for quick-service, 12–15 sq ft for a café or casual restaurant, and 18–20 sq ft for fine dining. The figure includes the seat, its share of the table and circulation space.

How many tables fit in a 1,000 sq ft café?

About 550–600 sq ft of a 1,000 sq ft café is usable dining area. At 14 sq ft per seat that's roughly 40 seats — around 10 four-tops, or a mix of 2-seaters and 4-seaters.

What's the best ratio of 2-seater to 4-seater tables?

Lead with 2-seaters since the average party is two, and add a smaller number of 4-seaters for groups. Push-together tops let you flex up for larger parties.

What size table is best for a café?

A 2 ft × 2 ft top seats two comfortably; a 4 ft × 2 ft top seats four. Fine-dining and bar tables run larger, usually 4 ft × 2.5 ft.
